20th Statistics Day and SDGs-National Indicator Framework Progress Report 2026

Source: PIB

Why in News?

The Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation (MoSPI) celebrated the 20th Statistics Day on 29 th June 2026 . The event was organized to commemorate the 133rd Birth Anniversary of Prof. P. C. Mahalanobis , recognizing his invaluable contribution to the national statistical system.

  • On this occasion, MoSPI also released the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) – National Indicator Framework Progress Report, 2026 , tracking India's progress towards the SDGs.

What is National Statistics Day?

  • National Statistics Day: The Government of India designated 29th June as 'Statistics Day' in 2007 . It serves as an annual tribute to the legacy of Prof. P.C. Mahalanobis.
  • Objective: To create public awareness, especially among the youth, about the role of statistics in socio-economic planning and policy formulation.
  • Theme for 2026: “Unlocking the Potential of Administrative Data.” It highlights the shift towards utilizing data generated through everyday government operations (like tax records, registries, and welfare enrollments) for evidence-based policymaking, rather than relying solely on traditional, resource-heavy sample surveys.

Prof. P.C. Mahalanobis

  • About: Prasanta Chandra Mahalanobis (1893–1972) is widely regarded as the Father of Indian Statistics . His visionary approach laid the foundation for India’s modern statistical architecture.
  • Key Contributions:
    • Institutional Builder: He founded the Indian Statistical Institute (ISI) in 1931 (declared an 'Institution of National Importance' by Parliament in 1959). Furthermore, his vision directly led to the establishment of the National Sample Survey (NSS) in 1950 and the Central Statistical Organisation (CSO) .
      • He also founded Sankhya , India's first statistical journal, in 1933 .
    • Architect of India's Economic Planning: He was a key member of the first Planning Commission of independent India.
      • He played a highly influential role in drafting India's Second Five-Year Plan (1956-1961) , which was fundamentally based on his renowned four-sector model (the Mahalanobis Model) that prioritized rapid, heavy industrialization and capital investment.
    • Mahalanobis Distance: Formulated in 1930, he introduced Mahalanobis Distance, a metric used to find the distance between a point and a distribution, widely used today in machine learning, cluster analysis, and classification techniques.
    • Large-Scale Sample Surveys: He pioneered the concept of using large-scale sample surveys for data collection, proving that a well-designed sample could provide highly accurate estimates for a massive population at a fraction of the cost of a census.
    • Practical Governance and Flood Control: A fierce advocate for applied statistics, his innovative data analysis was successfully utilized by the Indian Government to design effective flood prevention measures and yield important meteorological discoveries.
  • Global Recognition and National Honors: He served as the Chairman of the United Nations Subcommission on Statistical Sampling (1947–1951) , was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society, London (1945) , and was awarded India's second-highest civilian award, the Padma Vibhushan , in 1968 .

What are the Key Findings of the SDGs-National Indicator Framework Progress Report, 2026?

  • SDG 1 - No Poverty): Multidimensional poverty declined from 24.85% (2015-16) to 14.96% (2019-21) .
    • The population covered by social protection floors rose from 22.0% (2019) to 65.3% (2026) .
    • Key drivers: PM Jan Dhan Yojana , Direct Benefit Transfers.
  • SDG 2 - Zero Hunger: Child underweight (under 5 years) reduced from 35.7% (2015-16) to 31.8% (2023-24) .
    • Child stunting declined from 38.4% (2015-16) to 29.3% (2023-24) .
    • Key schemes: POSHAN Abhiyaan, and PM POSHAN .
  • SDG 3 - Good Health & Well-Being: Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR) improved from 122 (2015-17) to 87 (2022-24) per 1,00,000 live births.
    • Skilled health personnel birth attendance rose from 81.4% to 91.3% (NFHS-4 to NFHS-6).
    • The under-five mortality rate declined from 43 (2015) to 28 (2024) per 1,000 live births. Neonatal mortality rate fell from 25 (2015) to 18 (2024) per 1,000 live births.
    • TB treatment coverage improved significantly from 71% (2020) to 92% (2024) .
    • Key drivers: National Health Mission, Ayushman Bharat, Janani Suraksha Yojana, Mission Indradhanush.
  • SDG 4 - Quality Education: Gross Enrolment Ratio in higher secondary education rose from 48.32 (2015-16) to 48.32 (2024-25) .
    • Schools with internet facilities expanded from 46.30 (2015-16) to 84.20% (2024-25) .
    • Key enablers: Samagra Shiksha Abhiyan,  and National Education Policy 2020.
  • SDG 5 - Gender Equality: Sex Ratio at Birth improved from 896 (2015-17) to 918 (2022-24) .
    • Female-to-male Labour Force Participation Rate ratio improved from 0.43 (2020-21) to 0.52 (2025) .
    • Women-exclusive SHGs in bank-linked SHGs grew from 88.92% (2015-16) to 96.00% (2025-26) .
    • Key enablers: Beti Bachao Beti Padhao, SHG network expansion, and Gender Budgeting.
  • SDG 6 - Clean Water & Sanitation: Households with piped water supply (rural) rose from 48.15% (2021-22) to 81.81% (2025-26) .
    • Wastewater processed increased from 17.97% (2015-16) to 82.20% (2025-26) - a dramatic rise.
    • Water-use efficiency score improved from 2.89 USD/m³ (2020) to 5.30 USD/m³ (2023) .
    • Key missions: Jal Jeevan Mission , Swachh Bharat Mission, and Atal Bhujal Yojana .
  • SDG 7 - Affordable & Clean Energy: 100% household electrification achieved.
    • Renewable energy generating capacity grew from 64.04 watts per capita (2014-15) to 193.36 watts per capita (2025-26) , nearly 300% increase.
    • Energy intensity (primary energy per unit of GDP) declined from 0.2556 to 0.1329 MJ/Rupee (2015-16 to 2024-25), reflecting improved energy efficiency.
    • Key drivers: Saubhagya scheme , PM-KUSUM , PLI for Solar PV Modules.
  • SDG 8 - Decent Work & Economic Growth: Unemployment rate declined from 6.1% (2017-18) to 3.1% (2025) .
    • Tourism growth rate stood at 15.01% (2023-24) , reflecting sectoral recovery and expansion.
    • Key drivers: Startup India, PM Mudra Yojana, Swadesh Darshan 2.0 , e-Visa expansion.
  • SDG 9 - Industry, Innovation & Infrastructure: Manufacturing sector employment as a proportion of total employment improved from 11.40% (2022-23) to 12.14% (2025) .
    • Population covered by at least 4G mobile network reached 99.51% (2025) .
    • Key enablers: BharatNet , Atal Innovation Mission, Startup India .
  • SDG 10- Reduced Inequalities: Gini Coefficient for rural household expenditure improved from 0.28 (2011-12) to 0.23 (2023-24) , indicating lower inequality.
    • Urban Gini Coefficient also improved from 0.36 (2011-12) to 0.28 (2023-24) .
    • The ratio of female-to-male professional and technical workers improved to 51.3% (2025) , approaching parity.
    • Key instruments: Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana , PM-KISAN, Accessible India Campaign, Direct Benefit Transfers.
  • SDG 11 - Sustainable Cities & Communities: Percentage of wards with 100% door-to-door waste collection rose from 43 (2015-16) to 97 (2025-26) .
    • Percentage of waste processed rose from 17.97% (2015-16) to 82.20% (2025-26) .
    • India has adopted and implemented a national disaster risk reduction strategy aligned with the Sendai Framework (2015-2030).
    • Key initiatives: AMRUT, Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana (PMAY), Smart Cities Mission.
  • SDG 12 - Responsible Consumption & Production: Number of waste recycling plants installed grew from 829 (2019-20) to 3,236 (2025-26) .
    • Post-harvest storage losses of wheat and rice from central/state pool stocks declined nearly to zero ( -0.02 in 2025-26 ), reflecting better storage management.
    • Key policies: Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) frameworks for plastic waste, e-waste, batteries and tyres; circular economy promotion.
  • SDG 13 - Climate Action: India has updated Nationally Determined Contribution (2031-2035) and long-term strategies under the UNFCCC and Paris Agreement.
    • Compliance of industries with environmental standards improved from 86.25% (2018) to 91.82% (2024) .
    • Guided by NAPCC and its eight national missions; all States/UTs have prepared or updated State Action Plans on Climate Change.
  • SDG 14- Life Below Water: Maximum Sustainable Yield (MSY) in fishing increased from 3.078 million tonne/year (2015-16) to 5.311 million tonne/year (2025-26) .
    • India is in compliance with international laws for the conservation and sustainable use of oceans and marine resources.
    • Key interventions: Mangrove plantation, coral transplantation, shelterbelt development, artificial reefs, coral reef monitoring.
  • SDG 15 - Life on Land: Forest cover as a percentage of total geographical area increased from 21.34% (2019) to 21.76% (2023) .
    • The area of Ramsar (wetland) sites as a percentage of total wetland area more than doubled from 4.15% (2016) to 8.66% (2026) .
    • Key programmes: Green India Mission , Project Tiger , Project Elephant , Nagar Van Yojana, National Biodiversity Strategy & Action Plan (2024–2030) aligned with Global Biodiversity Framework).
  • SDG 16 - Peace, Justice & Strong Institutions: The number of judges increased from 1.33 per lakh population in 2016 to 1.55 in 2024, while the number of courts rose from 1.82 to 1.93 per lakh population during the same period.
    • Aadhaar coverage reached 95.67% in 2025–26, and India continues to maintain an independent National Human Rights Institution in compliance with the Paris Principles.
  • SDG 17 - Partnerships for the Goals :  India's share in global merchandise exports increased from 1.62% in 2015 to 1.70% in 2024.
    • Share in global commercial services exports rose significantly from 3.11% to 4.35% during the same period, reflecting the country's growing presence in global trade.

National Indicator Framework (NIF)

  • The National Indicator Framework (NIF) is the Government of India's primary statistical framework for monitoring progress towards the 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) .
  • Developed by MoSPI in consultation with NITI Aayog, UN agencies, and other stakeholders , it currently comprises 277 national indicators (2026) covering all SDGs.
  • The framework is periodically updated by the High-Level Steering Committee (HLSC) to align with the Global Indicator Framework (GIF) and national priorities.
  • It also supports localized SDG monitoring through State Indicator Frameworks (SIFs) and District Indicator Frameworks (DIFs) .

2025 Animal and Plant Discoveries and 111th Foundation Day of ZSI

Source: TH

Why in News?

The Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC) released the Animal Discoveries–2025 ( by Zoological Survey of India (ZSI)) and Plant Discoveries–2025 ( by Botanical Survey of India (BSI) ) compilations during the 111 th Foundation Day celebrations of the Zoological Survey of India in Kolkata.

What are the Key Highlights of the Animal and Plant Discoveries–2025?

Animal Discoveries–2025

  • India added 709 new species to its faunal database in 2025, bringing India's total documented faunal biodiversity to 1,05,953 species .
  • Regionally, Kerala led the discoveries with 98 new animal species , followed by West Bengal, Karnataka, and Arunachal Pradesh.
  • Among the most crucial vertebrates identified were a new Himalayan bat ( Myotis himalaicus ), Irwin’s wolf snake ( Lycodon irwini ), and two new species of green fan-throated lizards ( Ptyctolaemus mamdaphaensis and Ptyctolaemus siangensis ).

Plant Discoveries–2025

  • The BSI expanded the national floristic inventory by adding 353 plant taxa .  The discoveries highlighted a growing scientific focus on non-vascular organisms, which made up 57% of the additions, while the remaining 43% belonged to vascular plants .
  • State-wise, Arunachal Pradesh was the leading contributor with 49 floral discoveries , followed by Uttarakhand and Kerala.
  • Significant findings include a fern from Arunachal Pradesh ( Polystichum siangense ), a new wild relative of the custard apple from the Western Ghats ( Miliusa beddomei ), and a wild edible tooth-fungus ( Hericium indicum ).

What are the Key Facts About the Zoological Survey of India (ZSI)?

  • About: The Zoological Survey of India (ZSI) functions as the premier apex organization under the MoEFCC dedicated to comprehensive zoological research, faunal exploration, and animal taxonomy across the Indian subcontinent.
  • Administrative Structure: Headquartered in Kolkata, West Bengal , the ZSI operates a decentralized network of 16 regional centers situated across various distinct biogeographic zones of India, including the Western Ghats, marine, and high-altitude ecosystems.
  • Historical Timeline: The foundation of ZSI lies in the establishment of the Zoological Section of the Indian Museum in Calcutta in 1875. The broader vision traces back to Sir William Jones , who founded the Asiatic Society of Bengal in 1784.
    • The ZSI was formally established as an independent organization on 1 st July 1916 to survey and study the animal life of the then "British Indian Empire."
  • Expanded Legal Mandate: Following India’s ratification of the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) in 1994 , ZSI scaled up its operational mandate from pure taxonomic research to modern ecosystem-based conservation monitoring.
  • National Repository: Under the provisions of the Biological Diversity Act, 2002 , ZSI is officially designated as the supreme repository for the National Zoological Collection , housing millions of reference specimens.
  • Modern Initiatives: The organization plays a vital role in executing Environmental Impact Assessments (EIA) , developing DNA barcoding and wildlife forensics (such as the Pangolin Indexing System) to curb illicit trade.
  • Flagship Publications: To guide national policy and wildlife classification, ZSI periodically publishes the Indian Red Data Book , the Fauna of India series, and its highly referenced annual directory, Animal Discoveries .

Mandu’s Khurasani Imli Gets GI Tag

★ ★ ☆ ☆ ☆

Source: ET

Mandu’s(M.P) iconic Baobab fruit, locally known as Khurasani Imli, has received a Geographical Indication (GI) tag, giving it official recognition and protection.

  • About Khurasani Imli: It is a light green fruit with a tangy sweet-sour taste, known for its unique nutritional, medicinal, and cultural value in the Mandu region.
  • About the Baobab Tree: The Baobab tree, scientifically called Adansonia digitata , is famous for its huge water-storing trunk and inverted-tree-like branches, making it a major landmark of Mandu.
    • Historical Origin: The tree is believed to have been introduced to Mandu nearly six centuries ago by Afghan and Arab traders and later adapted to the local climate and terrain.
    • Local Concentration: Mandu has India’s largest naturally occurring population of Baobab trees, with officials estimating more than 1,000 trees in the region.
    • Traditional Uses: Tribal communities have long used the fruit pulp, juice, seeds, and dried bark for health purposes, including treating diabetes, digestive problems, fever, and fatigue.
  • Impact: The tag is expected to benefit tribal families through better income, organised marketing, cooperatives, value-added products, new plantations, and conservation , though dedicated government conservation programmes and scientific research are still lacking.
  • Significance: The GI tag is expected to improve branding, marketing and value-added industries around Baobab-based products, directly benefiting tribal families involved in collection and processing.

Delhi Electric Vehicle Policy 2.0

★ ★ ☆ ☆ ☆

Source: TH

Why in News?

Recently, the Delhi government notified its new Electric Vehicle Policy 2.0 , with the policy likely to come into effect from 1st July 2026 and remain valid till 31st March 2030 .

What are the Key Provisions of Delhi Electric Vehicle Policy 2.0?

  • About: The policy aims to make Delhi a leading electric mobility hub by focusing on high-polluting and high-use vehicle segments such as two-wheelers, three-wheelers, commercial goods vehicles, school buses, aggregator fleets and government transport fleets.
  • Target: It aims to achieve at least 30% electrification of Delhi’s total vehicle fleet by 31st March 2030, building on the success of the EV policy  2020, under which Delhi achieved around 14% EV penetration by 2025.
  • Mandates: From 1st January 2027, only electric three-wheelers and N1 category trucks will be registered in Delhi; from 1st April 2028, only electric two-wheelers will be registered. Already registered fossil-fuel vehicles will be allowed to complete their full lifecycle.
  • Buses: School buses will have to convert at least 10% of their fleet to EVs within two years of notification of the policy , with a broader target of 30% electrification by 2030.
  • Funding: The government has allocated ₹7,000 crore for policy implementation, with an overall investment plan of ₹15,000 crore, including charging infrastructure, purchase incentives and scrapping incentives.
  • Incentives: EV buyers will get purchase incentives, road tax exemption and registration fee waiver. Two-wheelers will receive incentives up to ₹30,000 in the first year, ₹20,000 in the second year and ₹10,000 in the third year. Three-wheelers will receive ₹50,000, ₹40,000 and ₹30,000 respectively, while N1 commercial trucks will get a subsidy up to ₹1 lakh in the first year.
  • Scrapping: Scrapping incentives will be provided for vehicles below BS-IV emission norms , including around ₹10,000 for two-wheelers, ₹25,000 for three-wheelers, ₹1 lakh for four-wheelers, ₹50,000 for N1 trucks and ₹15,000 for Gramin Seva units completing their lifecycle.
  • Infrastructure: The policy plans to install more than 30,000 public charging points, with around ₹1,000 crore allocated for charging infrastructure.
  • Implementation Agencies: The Transport Department will act as the nodal agency for implementing the policy, while Delhi Transco Ltd. will develop charging infrastructure in coordination with the power department and distribution companies.
  • Exemption: Electric N2 trucks carrying loads between 3.5 and 12 tonnes will be exempted from “No Entry” timings for 10 years , but this benefit will apply only to the first 1,000 N2 trucks purchased within three months of policy notification.
  • Hybrids: The policy clearly excludes hybrid vehicles from subsidies, as the government wants to focus only on pure EVs because of their zero-emission benefits.

Gitchak nakana: First Blind Aquifer-Dwelling Fish in Northeast India

★ ★ ☆ ☆ ☆

Source: TOI

An international team of taxonomists discovered a new genus and species of blind, aquifer-dwelling fish named Gitchak nakana from a dug-out well in Assam , marking the first such record in Northeast India and the Eastern Himalayan landscape .

  • About the Species ( Gitchak nakana ):
    • Nomenclature: The name is derived from the Garo language , where ‘Gitchak’ means red (referring to the fish's striking red life color), and ‘na-tok kana’ translates to 'blind fish' (due to the absence of eyes ).
    • Taxonomy: It belongs to a new genus within the family Cobitidae (a family of true loaches).
    • Habitat: It is a highly specialized subterranean (groundwater-dwelling) fish, found in extremely low numbers in a single dug-out well. The exact location was withheld to protect the vulnerable population.
  • Geographical and Ecological Significance: This discovery provides the first clear evidence that the Eastern Himalayas harbor highly specialized subterranean phreatobitic (aquifer-dwelling) fauna .
    • Prior to this discovery, such evolutionarily distinct subterranean fish were primarily known only from the lateritic lowlands of the Western Ghats in southern India.
    • Subterranean fishes , such as the Blind Catfish ( Horaglanis krishnai ) of Kerala, are among the world's most evolutionarily distinct fishes , specially adapted to life in underground aquatic habitats.
  • Conservation Concerns & Related Species: The research highlighted the urgent need for conservation prioritization for subterranean species with restricted distributions.
    • Experts raised alarms about Neolissochilus pnar (a cave mahseer from Meghalaya , recognized as the world's largest cavefish ), which is facing severe threats from the international aquarium pet trade despite insufficient data on its population size.
