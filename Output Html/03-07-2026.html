For Mains: India-Japan Relations, Strategic Partnerships and Regional Security, Key Challenges and Way Forward in India-Japan Relations

Source: PIB

Why in News?

The 16th India-Japan Annual Summit was held in New Delhi.  The summit provided a platform to review and strengthen the "Special Strategic and Global Partnership" between the two nations, with a focus on defense co-development, clean energy, and artificial intelligence.

Summary

  • The 16th India-Japan Annual Summit significantly strengthened the Special Strategic and Global Partnership through cooperation in defence co-development, economic security, AI, clean energy, resilient supply chains, and a rules-based Indo-Pacific.
  • While the partnership continues to deepen, addressing challenges such as underperforming trade, infrastructure delays, non-tariff barriers, and defence technology transfer through institutional reforms and greater strategic cooperation will be crucial for realizing its full potential.

What are the Key Outcomes of the 16th India-Japan Annual Summit?

  • Defence and Security Cooperation: India and Japan achieved a breakthrough by reaching an agreement in principle on the remaining technical aspects of the UNICORN (Unified Complex Radio Antenna) project, marking their first defence co-development programme .
    • The two sides also agreed to expand defence equipment collaboration, strengthen maritime security, naval Maintenance, Repair, and Overhaul (MRO), Maritime Domain Awareness (MDA) , joint military exercises, and hold the 4th India-Japan 2+2 Ministerial Meeting later in 2026.
  • Economic Security and Resilient Supply Chains: The summit adopted the India-Japan Joint Declaration on Economic Security Cooperation , focusing on semiconductors, critical minerals, ICT, clean energy, and pharmaceuticals.
    • Both countries committed to building resilient and diversified supply chains, reducing dependence on any single country, and addressing economic coercion and export restrictions.
  • Energy Security and Clean Energy Partnership: India and Japan adopted a Joint Statement on Energy Resilience and agreed to deepen cooperation on strategic petroleum reserves, maritime energy transport, and stable energy supply chains.
    • Launched the India-Japan Cooperative Biogas for Growth (CBG) Initiative to support India's target of establishing 1,000 biogas and organic fertilizer plants.
    • The two countries also expanded cooperation in green hydrogen, clean ammonia, solar PV technologies, and nuclear energy, while Japan reaffirmed its support for India's membership of the International Energy Agency (IEA) .
  • Artificial Intelligence and Emerging Technologies: Launched the first India-Japan AI Strategic Dialogue and adopted a Joint Statement on AI Cooperation .
    • Agreed to collaborate on trusted AI, resilient digital infrastructure, AI supply chains, and responsible AI governance.
  • Trade, Investment and Industrial Cooperation: Both sides agreed to accelerate the review of the Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement (CEPA) and work towards achieving the ¥ 10 trillion Japanese investment target .
    • A Memorandum of Cooperation (MoC) was signed for a USD 10 Billion investment package, alongside the facilitation of Japanese financial institutions in India's banking sector.
    • Cooperation will expand under the India-Japan Industrial Competitiveness Partnership (IJICP) in logistics, agriculture, healthcare, textiles, industrial manufacturing, payment systems, MSMEs and startups through initiatives such as the India-Japan SME Forum and JISSI .
  • Indo-Pacific and Global Strategic Cooperation: The summit reaffirmed commitment to a Free and Open Indo-Pacific (FOIP) aligned with India's Indo-Pacific Oceans Initiative (IPOI) and MAHASAGAR vision.
    • Both countries strengthened cooperation through the Quad , ASEAN and BIMSTEC, supported freedom of navigation under UNCLOS , backed UN Security Council reforms , enhanced cooperation on disaster risk reduction, and reiterated a strong joint commitment to combating terrorism and promoting a rules-based international order.
    • Announced the initiation of a 1.5 track policy dialogue alongside the Philippines, signaling a united front on Indo-Pacific maritime security.
  • Maritime Security: Expressed concern over developments in the East China Sea and South China Sea , opposed unilateral attempts to change the status quo, and reaffirmed commitment to UNCLOS , freedom of navigation, and peaceful dispute resolution.
  • Disaster Management: Japan will support India in hosting the 4th UN World Conference on Disaster Risk Reduction in 2030.
  • Africa Cooperation: Agreed to deepen collaboration in Africa through the India-Africa Forum Summit (IAFS) , and the India-Japan Cooperation Initiative for Sustainable Economic Development in Africa .

How are India- Japan Relations?

  • Evolution of India–Japan Relations: India and Japan share civilizational links through Buddhism . Diplomatic relations were established in 1952 , followed by the elevation of ties to a Global Partnership (2000) , the launch of the Annual Summit mechanism (2006) , the signing of the Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement (CEPA) in 2011 , the upgrade to a Special Strategic and Global Partnership (2014) , culminating in the Japan–India Vision 2025 for peace and prosperity in the Indo-Pacific .
    • The establishment of the India-Japan Act East Forum(2017) alongside the revival of the Quad in 2017 , reflecting the steady expansion of bilateral cooperation beyond economics into strategic and security domains.
  • Defense and Security Cooperation: Security ties have been institutionalized through mechanisms like the 2+2 Ministerial Dialogue (Foreign and Defense Ministers) and the Joint Declaration on Security Cooperation.
    • The Reciprocal Provision of Supplies and Services Agreement (2020), agreement facilitates deep logistical support during joint military exercises (e.g., JIMEX , Dharma Guardian , Malabar , and Veer Guardian ).
    • Defence ties were strengthened through the Transfer of Defence Equipment and Technology Agreement (2015), and the 2024 UNICORN mast co-development project for advanced stealth capabilities in naval vessels.
  • Economic and Commercial Relations: India–Japan bilateral trade reached USD 27.48 billion in FY 2025–26 , with India's exports at USD 6.04 billion and imports at USD 21.44 billion. Japan is th e 5th largest source of FDI in India.
    • India is currently Japan’s 14th largest trading partner, whereas Japan is India’s 10th largest trading partner.
    • India's major exports include organic chemicals, vehicles, aluminium products, and fish, while major imports comprise electrical machinery, nuclear reactors, copper, inorganic chemicals, and iron & steel.
    • Post-pandemic geopolitical realities have pushed both nations toward the Economic Security Initiative focused heavily on building resilient global supply chains in critical minerals, semiconductors, clean energy, and pharmaceuticals to avoid over-reliance on any single country (China).
    • Japan remains the largest bilateral donor to India. Japanese Official Development Assistance (ODA) funds critical infrastructure, most notably the Mumbai-Ahmedabad High-Speed Rail (MAHSR) project and various metro networks.
    • Japan also continues to support flagship infrastructure projects, including the Delhi Metro , while collaborating on India's vision to develop a 7,000-km high-speed rail network by 2047 .
  • North-East Connectivity: The India–Japan Act East Forum (2017) aligns India's Act East Policy with Japan's Free and Open Indo-Pacific (FOIP) vision to develop North-East India as a connectivity hub linking South Asia and Southeast Asia , supported by Japan International Cooperation Agency (JICA)- funded infrastructure projects , including the 750-km North East Road Network Connectivity Improvement Project .
  • Technology, and Space: ISRO and JAXA are jointly developing the Lunar Polar Exploration (LUPEX) Mission to explore the moon's south pole by 2026-27.
    • The India-Japan Digital Partnership focuses on AI, IoT, and semiconductors, supported by robust academic pipelines like the Sakura and LOTUS exchange programs.
  • Human Capital and Cultural Exchange: Initiatives like the Technical Intern Training Programme (TITP) and Specified Skilled Worker (SSW) facilitate the movement of Indian blue-collar workers and professionals to Japan.
    • Approximately 59,000 Indians reside in Japan, with a growing demographic of IT professionals, engineers, and merchants.
  • Strategic Balancing: Despite China being the largest trading partner of both India and Japan , the two countries are deepening cooperation in semiconductors, critical minerals, and renewable energy to reduce supply chain risks, while also strengthening their strategic partnership amid uncertainty over US security and trade commitments in the Indo-Pacific.

What are the Challenges in India-Japan Relations?

  • Underperforming Trade Potential: Despite CEPA, bilateral trade (~USD 27 billion) remains heavily skewed in Japan's favor and is vastly underperforming compared to India-China or Japan-China trade volumes.
  • Execution Delays: Flagship infrastructure projects, particularly the MAHSR, India's first bullet train project, have faced delays due to land acquisition challenges and state-level political friction, slowing implementation despite continued bilateral commitment.
    • Despite being launched as a joint alternative to China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) , the Asia-Africa Growth Corridor (AAGC) has seen limited progress due to the absence of a robust joint financing mechanism, constraining infrastructure cooperation in the Global South.
  • Business Environment Hurdles: Japanese investors often cite bureaucratic bottlenecks, complex labor laws, and unpredictable regulatory frameworks in India as deterrents to scaling up manufacturing.
  • Non-Tariff Barriers (NTBs): Indian exports, particularly in pharmaceuticals (generic drugs) and agriculture, face stringent Phytosanitary (SPS) measures and complex regulatory hurdles in the Japanese market.
  • Demographic Dividend vs. Language Barrier: While Japan faces a severe labor shortage and India has a demographic dividend , the language barrier and strict Japanese immigration/work culture slow the effective utilization of mechanisms like the Specified Skilled Worker (SSW) program.
  • Strategic Differences: Divergent approaches towards Russia and Eurasian security persist, with Japan aligning with the G7 sanctions regime while India follows strategic autonomy and maintains close defence and energy ties with Russia.
  • Defence Technology Transfer Constraints: Japan's post-World War II pacifist constitution led to strict restrictions on defence exports and technology transfer.
    • This has slowed major defence deals, including the ShinMaywa US-2 amphibious aircraft project, posing challenges for deeper defence industrial cooperation under India's Atmanirbhar Bharat initiative.
    • However, Japan’s decision to scrap its ban on lethal weapons exports marks a major policy shift. This reversal could clear the way for stalled projects and significantly boost future defence technology transfers between the two nations.

What Measures are Needed to Strengthen India-Japan Relations?

  • Negotiate Mutual Recognition Agreements (MRAs): Establish MRAs to reduce non-tariff barriers (NTBs) under the CEPA , enabling easier access for Indian pharmaceuticals and agricultural products to the Japanese market.
  • Create a Joint Sovereign Infrastructure Fund: Establish a dedicated India–Japan financing mechanism to accelerate projects like the Asia–Africa Growth Corridor (AAGC) and strategic infrastructure in the Global South.
  • Strengthen the Skilled Workforce Pipeline: Develop Centres of Excellence (CoEs) under Japan's Specified Skilled Worker (SSW) programme to train Indian talent in advanced manufacturing, semiconductors, and precision engineering.
  • Expand Strategic Minilateral Partnerships: Deepen issue-based cooperation through India–Japan–Philippines (maritime security) and India–Japan–Australia (critical minerals) frameworks to strengthen Indo-Pacific resilience and bypass the institutional paralysis of larger forums like ASEAN or SAARC.
  • Cultural Diplomacy 2.0: Move beyond "Buddhism and Yoga." Establish dedicated linguistic and technical training institutes in India tailored specifically to Japanese corporate standards to truly leverage the SSW migration corridor.
    • Both governments are targeting the movement of 5 lakh Indian students and workers to Japan over the next five years , leveraging India's demographic dividend to address Japan's ageing population and labour shortages .

Conclusion

The India-Japan relationship has transcended traditional bilateral boundaries to become a pillar of stability in the Indo-Pacific. By transitioning from "shared values" to "shared economic and security interests," the partnership is well-poised to shape the geopolitical landscape of the 21st century.

VIJAY Roadmap for Indian Army

★ ★ ☆ ☆ ☆

Source: TH

Chief of the Army Staff General Dhiraj Seth has assumed charge as the 31 st Chief of the Army Staff and unveiled the "VIJAY" roadmap for the Indian Army's modernization.

  • Aligned with the Ministry of Defence's Decade of Transformation (2023–2032), the roadmap aims to build a digitized, technology-enabled, self-reliant (Aatmanirbhar), and multi-domain capable force.
  • The roadmap reflects the Prime Minister's mantra that "JAI leads to VIJAY", with "Jai Hind" and "Jai Bharat" as its guiding ethos.

Core Pillars of the 'VIJAY' Roadmap

  • Vigilance (V): Prioritizes heightened operational readiness and constant alertness along India's borders to effectively counter evolving security dynamics.
  • Innovation and Transformation (I): Focuses on the integration of modern military doctrine, cutting-edge technologies, and rapid capability development to adapt to the changing character of warfare.
  • Jointness and Integration (J): Emphasizes deeper structural synergy among the Army, Navy, and Air Force , alongside military-civil fusion through a Whole-of-Nation approach to achieve the vision of Viksit Bharat 2047 .
  • Atmanirbharta (A): Reaffirms the armed forces' absolute commitment to indigenization under the core guiding principle of “Winning Our Wars with Indigenous Solutions” , driving defense self-reliance.
  • Yodha First (Y): Centers on human capital development, prioritizing enhanced training standards, technological skilling, and welfare frameworks for everyone from Agniveers to veterans and Veer Naris.

Centre Amends Drugs Rules 1945 for Advanced Therapies

★ ★ ☆ ☆ ☆

Source: TH

The Central Government has amended the Drugs Rules, 1945 to bring cell/stem cell-derived products , gene therapeutic products, and xenografts under the Centrally License Approving Authority (CLAA) framework, strengthening the regulation of advanced medical technologies and ensuring uniform national standards.

  • Objective: The amendment seeks to strengthen patient safety, ensure uniform regulatory standards across States, and promote the safe adoption of advanced medical technologies through joint Central and State oversight.
  • Centrally Licence Approving Authority (CLAA): Functioning under the Central Drugs Standard Control Organization (CDSCO) and empowered by the Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940 , the CLAA is a joint licensing mechanism.
    • It ensures the uniform regulation and oversight of critical drugs and biological products through the collaborative efforts of both Central and State Licensing Authorities.
  • Expansion of CLAA Framework: Previously covering vaccines, large volume parenterals (IV solutions >100 ml), and recombinant DNA (r-DNA) medicines , the framework has now been expanded to regulate advanced cell and gene therapies .
  • Cell or Stem Cell-derived Products: These include stem cell-based regenerative therapies and CAR-T cell therapies , which are increasingly used to treat blood cancers such as leukemia and lymphoma .
  • Gene Therapeutic Products: These include gene replacement and gene-editing therapies used in the treatment of genetic disorders and several types of cancers .
  • Xenografts: Xenografts are animal tissue-derived products , such as heart valves , transplanted into humans and widely used in cardiology and orthopedics .
  • Need for Enhanced Regulation: Since these therapies involve complex, specialized, and rapidly evolving medical technologies , stricter regulatory oversight is essential to ensure their quality, safety, and efficacy .
    • The amendment strengthens India's regulatory framework , aligns it with global best practices , promotes innovation in healthcare , and facilitates the safe and timely adoption of cutting-edge medical technologies .

GAGAN: India’s Indigenous Satellite-Based Navigation System

★ ★ ☆ ☆ ☆

Source: PIB

Recently, the Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA) successfully conducted India's first satellite-based landing system approach on a commercial jet aircraft using GAGAN , marking a major milestone in indigenous aviation navigation.

  • About: GAGAN ( GPS Aided GEO Augmented Navigation ) is India's indigenous Satellite-Based Augmentation System (SBAS) , jointly developed by ISRO and the Airports Authority of India (AAI) . It has been operational since 2015 and enhances GPS accuracy and integrity for safer navigation.
  • Need: Standard GPS signals can be affected by atmospheric and other errors, making them unsuitable for precision aviation. GAGAN provides real-time corrections and integrity information (alerts when GPS signals are unreliable), enabling safer aircraft navigation and satellite-based landing.
  • Key Features: GAGAN improves GPS accuracy, provides integrity monitoring, supports precision approach and landing , is certified to international civil aviation standards, and is the first SBAS certified for the equatorial region.
  • Architecture: GAGAN operates through an integrated network of 15 Indian Reference Stations (INRES), 2 Indian Master Control Centres (INMCC), 3 Indian Land Uplink Stations (INLUS), 4 communication networks, and 3 geostationary satellites (GSAT-8, GSAT-10 and GSAT-15) carrying GAGAN payloads.
  • Applications: Beyond aviation, GAGAN supports maritime navigation, road transport, railways, disaster management, defence, telecommunications, surveying and mapping .
  • Significance: GAGAN has placed India among a select group of countries with an operational SBAS (alongside the USA, Europe and Japan ) and is interoperable with WAAS (USA), EGNOS (Europe), and MSAS (Japan).
  • GAGAN vs NavIC: GAGAN is an SBAS that augments GPS through corrections and integrity information for precision aviation, whereas NavIC is India's indigenous regional navigation satellite system providing positioning, navigation and timing (PNT) services over India and up to 1,500 km beyond its boundaries.

Reforms to Expand Foreign Participation in G-Secs

★ ★ ☆ ☆ ☆

Source: PIB

Why in News?

The Government has introduced reforms to expand Foreign Portfolio Investor (FPI) participation in Government Securities (G-Secs) , aiming to deepen India’s bond market, attract long-term foreign capital, and strengthen India’s position as a leading global investment destination.

What are the Key Reforms Introduced to Expand Foreign Participation in Government Securities (G-Secs)?

  • Objective: To deepen India’s bond market, diversify the investor base, attract stable long-term foreign capital , and strengthen the debt market.
  • Taxation: Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs) and Foreign Institutional Investors (FIIs) are exempt from interest income and capital gains from the sale, transfer, exchange, or redemption of Government Securities (G-Secs), effective from 1 April 2026.
  • Expansion: The Fully Accessible Route (FAR) has been expanded to include new issuances of 15-year, 30-year, and 40-year Government Securities (G-Secs), along with Sovereign Green Bonds (SGrBs) in FAR-eligible tenors.
    • The FAR allows foreign investors to invest in select Government Securities (G-Secs) without the restrictions applicable under the General Route.
  • Liberalisation: Under the General Route, the Government has removed the short-term investment limit, concentration limit, and security-wise investment limit .
  • Limits: Overall investment limits remain unchanged at 6% of outstanding Central Government Securities (CGSs) and 2% of outstanding State Government Securities (SGSs).
  • Rationalisation: The existing General and Long-Term investment categories for Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs) have been merged into a single investment limit for Central and State Government Securities.
  • Investors: The reforms aim to attract long-term institutional investors such as pension funds, insurance companies, and sovereign wealth funds.
  • Significance: These reforms are expected to improve market liquidity, price discovery, yield curve development, financial market benchmarks, and monetary policy transmission, while reducing government borrowing costs.
    • Greater foreign participation can provide additional funding for infrastructure, manufacturing, urban development, climate initiatives, and other national priorities, while supporting inclusion of Indian bonds in global indices and strengthening India’s integration with global financial markets.

Key Concepts Related to Foreign Investment in Government Securities

  • Foreign Institutional Investor (FII): A category of Foreign Portfolio Investor (FPI) comprising institutional investors such as mutual funds, pension funds, insurance companies, and hedge funds that invest pooled funds and play an active role in investment decisions.
  • Foreign Portfolio Investor (FPI): Foreign individuals, institutions, or funds investing in financial assets such as stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and Government Securities (G-Secs) without participating in the management or control of the investee entities.
  • Government Securities (G-Secs): Tradable debt instruments issued by the Central or State Governments to finance public expenditure, manage fiscal deficits, and regulate market liquidity .
  • Bank for International Settlements (BIS) : An international financial institution owned by central banks that promotes global monetary and financial cooperation and also functions as a banker and asset manager for central banks and international organisations.
  • Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG): Capital gains arising from the transfer of Government Securities held for more than 12 months (listed G-Secs) or more than 24 months (unlisted G-Secs) .
  • Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG): Capital gains arising from the transfer of Government Securities held for up to 12 months (listed G-Secs) or up to 24 months (unlisted G-Secs) .
