India–Italy Special Strategic Partnership

For Prelims: Food and Agriculture Organization , India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor , Agricola Medal, European Union

For Mains: India’s evolving strategic partnerships with Europe, Significance of the India–Italy Special Strategic Partnership, IMEC and India’s connectivity diplomacy

Source:PIB

Why in News?

The Prime Minister of India paid an official visit to Italy. During this visit, the two nations officially elevated their bilateral relations to a Special Strategic Partnership.

  • Additionally, during his visit to Rome, Prime Minister Narendra Modi was conferred with the prestigious Agricola Medal for 2026 by the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) in recognition of his efforts toward global food security and sustainable agriculture.

Summary

  • India and Italy elevated their ties to a Special Strategic Partnership , expanding cooperation in trade, defence, AI, critical minerals, maritime security, connectivity, and the India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor to strengthen the emerging Indo-Mediterranean strategic space.
  • Prime Minister Narendra Modi was also awarded the prestigious Food and Agriculture Organization Agricola Medal 2026 for India’s achievements in food security, sustainable agriculture, climate-resilient farming, and digital agricultural innovation.

What are the Key Highlights of the PM’s Visit to Italy?

  • Political & Strategic Elevation: Relations have been upgraded from a Strategic Partnership to a Special Strategic Partnership.
    • A new Foreign Ministers-led mechanism will be established to review the Joint Strategic Action Plan 2025-2029 (adopted at the G20 Summit held in Rio de Janeiro in November 2024 ).
  • Economic Cooperation & Trade Targets: With the successful conclusion of negotiations for the EU–India Free Trade Agreement, both nations set an ambitious bilateral trade target of EUR 20 billion by 2029 .
    • Priority sectors include clean technologies, semiconductors, critical minerals, and pharmaceuticals.
  • Defence and Security Collaboration: A Defence Industrial Roadmap was adopted to promote co-development and co-production projects, specifically targeting helicopters, naval platforms, and electronic warfare.
    • Both leaders also agreed to launch a formal Dialogue on Maritime Security.
  • Science and Technology: The leaders announced the creation of INNOVIT India , an innovation hub to connect startups, universities, and industries.
    • They also reaffirmed their commitment to human-centric Artificial Intelligence, building on the outcomes of the AI Impact Summit 2026 held in New Delhi.
  • Critical Minerals & Energy: A Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) was signed to establish a structured framework for securing critical minerals , with an emphasis on recycling electronic waste.
    • Cooperation will also deepen in green hydrogen , smart grids, and renewable energy.
  • Connectivity: Reaffirmed their commitment to the India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor (IMEC) as a transformative connectivity initiative linking India, the Middle East, and Europe.
    • Both countries emphasized that IMEC would improve trade routes, energy connectivity, logistics, and resilient supply chains while strengthening the emerging “Indo-Mediterranean” strategic space.
  • Migration and Mobility: Both sides agreed to enhance the mobility of skilled professionals particularly in STEM sectors and nursing through frameworks like the "ICI - Italy Calls India" talent bridge.
  • Cultural Exchanges: The year 2027 will be celebrated as the "Year of Culture and Tourism between Italy and India."

Agricola Medal

  • Agricola Medal: It is the highest award conferred by the FAO Director-General upon extraordinary global leaders who exhibit tangible commitment and action toward eradicating hunger, reducing poverty, and achieving the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) .
  • Recognition of Indian Agriculture: The award highlights India's science-driven agricultural approach.
    • The FAO lauded India's farmer-centric initiatives, specifically highlighting the implementation of the world's largest food-based social safety net covering 800 million people and the provisioning of direct income support to more than 110 million farmers (via PM-KISAN ).
    • The award commends India’s structural shift toward regenerative and natural farming , alongside its global leadership in promoting nutrient-dense, climate-resilient crops, culminating in the International Year of Millets 2023 celebrated in partnership with the FAO.
      • Key achievements recognized also include the use of Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) in farming.
  • Alignment with the 'Four Betters' Framework: India reaffirmed its commitment to the FAO’s core operational roadmap aimed at driving agrifood systems transformation: Better Production, Better Nutrition, Better Environment, and a Better Life , ensuring no one is left behind.

What is the Significance of Italy for India?

  • Western Anchor of the IMEC: With the Red Sea and Suez Canal facing persistent security volatilities, the IMEC offers a multi-modal alternative.
    • Italy’s geographical projection into the Mediterranean makes it the logical, and most willing, European entry point for Indian goods, energy, and data cables traversing the Middle East.
    • Italy’s strategic withdrawal from China’s Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) in late 2023 created a vacuum that India is strategically filling.
      • By anchoring the IMEC, India uses Italy to structurally integrate the Indo-Pacific with the Mediterranean , countering Chinese logistical hegemony in Southern Europe.
  • Pillar for Defence Indigenization: Italy possesses advanced, niche military technologies that are highly complementary to India’s "Make in India" defense objectives, particularly as India seeks to diversify away from its historical dependence on Russian hardware.
    • Italian defense expertise in radar systems, electronic warfare, and helicopters is crucial for modernizing India's armed forces without the threat of Western sanctions (such as CAATSA ) that accompany Russian or highly restricted American tech.
  • Geopolitical Leverage Within the EU: Post- Brexit, India requires strong advocates within the EU to navigate Brussels' complex regulatory and trade environments.
    • While France is India’s closest strategic partner in Europe, relying solely on Paris is risky. Italy, as the EU’s third-largest economy, provides New Delhi with a vital balancing weight within the European Parliament and Council, particularly aiding the India-EU Free Trade Agreement .
    • Italy’s support is crucial for India in pushing back against protectionist EU policies, such as the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), ensuring that India's industrial exports are not unfairly penalized.
  • Technological Sovereignty and the Green Transition: Italy is a global leader in precision manufacturing, robotics, and circular economy practices, aligning perfectly with India’s domestic industrial targets.
    • While India lacks domestic reserves of rare earth elements, Italy holds advanced technologies for extracting critical minerals from electronic waste.
    • Partnering with Italy allows India to build a resilient, circular supply chain for semiconductors and EVs, mitigating Beijing’s monopoly on critical mineral processing.
  • Bridging the Global North and Global South: Italy’s "Mattei Plan" seeks a non-predatory, developmental approach to Africa.
    • This synergizes with India’s extensive developmental footprint (via Digital Public Infrastructure and lines of credit) on the African continent.
    • Together, India and Italy can offer a transparent, capacity-building alternative to African nations burdened by debt-trap diplomacy of China.

India-Italy Relations

  • Trade Architecture: Italy stands as India's 4th largest trading partner within the European Union (EU) , with bilateral merchandise trade reaching USD 13.76 billion in 2024-25 .  India consistently maintains a positive trade balance (USD 1.70 billion in 2024-25).
  • Foreign Direct Investment (FDI): Italy ranks 19 th in cumulative FDI inflows to India (April 2000 – March 2025).
    • The capital inflow is heavily concentrated in the Automobile sector (29.8%) , followed by Trading (17.1%) and Industrial Machinery (5.6%) .
  • Institutionalized Investment Support: Following high-level forums in early 2025, Italy announced EUR 470 million in new investments and established a dedicated EUR 500 million funding line via SIMEST (the Italian agency for SME internationalization) by opening its office in Delhi.
  • Strategic Defence and Maritime Convergence: India shares a deeply rooted military legacy with Italy dating back to the Italian Campaign of World War II , where six Victoria Crosses were awarded to Indian soldiers out of 20 total decorations.
    • Maritime and Naval Interoperability: Bilateral naval coordination features regular PASSEX (Passing Exercises) , alongside Italian participation in the MILAN Naval Exercises .
  • Science, Technology: Indian researchers have secured direct access to elite European scientific installations via a Letter of Intent signed with the Elettra Sincrotrone facility in Trieste, Italy .
    • Under coordination by ISRO and the Italian Space Agency (ASI) , the partnership has integrated commercial operations through B2B agreements covering satellite services and ground station solutions .
  • Counter-Terrorism & Cyber Security: Institutional alignment against asymmetric threats includes the launch of the Inaugural Bilateral Cyber Dialogue focusing on the protection of critical information infrastructure , alongside active coordination within the Joint Working Group on Counter Terrorism (JWG-CT) .
  • Geopolitices : Italy joined several India- led plurilateral initiatives such as International Solar Alliance (ISA ), Indian Ocean Rim Association (IORA), Coalition for Disaster Resilient Infrastructure (CDRI) and Indo-Pacific Oceans Initiative (IPOI).

What are the Challenges in India-Italy Relations?

  • Geopolitical Vulnerability : The physical infrastructure of IMEC remains highly vulnerable to ongoing geopolitical volatility in West Asia.
    • Proxy conflicts, the Red Sea crisis, and threats to maritime choke points (like the Strait of Hormuz) present severe security and financial risks that threaten to delay the operationalization of both the maritime and rail segments of the corridor.
  • Protectionist Policies: European regulatory frameworks, particularly the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) and strict sanitary and phytosanitary (SPS) measures, disproportionately impact Indian exports (such as steel, textiles, and agricultural products), slowing down true commercial integration.
  • Legacy Compliance: Both nations suffer from complex, legacy procurement and compliance systems within their respective defense ministries.
    • Bureaucratic red tape, rigid technology transfer negotiations, and delays in finalizing joint ventures between Italian defense giants (like Leonardo or Fincantieri) and Indian private/public sector firms often stall active production lines.
  • Differing Frameworks: Scaling joint ventures in Artificial Intelligence, fintech, and digital public infrastructure requires aligning India's data localization mandates (such as the Digital Personal Data Protection Act,2023 ) with Europe's highly stringent General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) .
    • Bridging these differing approaches to data sovereignty remains a complex legal hurdle.
  • Financial Burden: Without a Social Security Agreement (SSA) in place, Indian expatriates and professionals working in Italy often face double taxation or lose their pension contributions when they return to India.
    • Reconciling conflicting tax structures and ensuring pension portability takes years of intense negotiation and directly impacts the attractiveness of the Italian labor market for Indian talent.

What Measures can Strengthen India-Italy Relations?

  • Focus on Dual-Use Technologies: Beyond traditional hardware, the Defence Industrial Roadmap must prioritize joint R&D in emerging domains such as Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) for secure naval communications and fiber-optic drone swarms, ensuring interoperability in the Indo-Pacific.
    • Elevate Italy's role from a liaison at the Information Fusion Centre for Indian Ocean Region (IFC-IOR) in Gurugram to a joint developer of autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) and anti-submarine warfare (ASW) tech, specifically leveraging Italy's Fincantieri for India's Project 75I.
  • Green Tech Transfer Mechanism: India and Italy should establish a bilateral framework to facilitate the transfer of Italian decarbonization technologies to Indian MSMEs.
    • This will help Indian exports (especially steel and textiles) remain competitive against the EU’s CBAM .
  • Data Adequacy Framework: To operationalise the INNOVIT India hub, the two nations must negotiate a "data equivalence" pact that bridges the compliance gap between India’s DPDP Act and the EU’s strict GDPR , enabling seamless cross-border data flows for AI and fintech startups.
    • India and Italy must co-author global governance frameworks for AI ("Algor-ethics") to ensure that emerging regulatory architectures (like the EU AI Act) do not stifle the developmental needs of the Global South.
  • Expediting the Social Security Agreement (SSA): Finalizing SSA agreement is critical to exempting Indian STEM professionals and nurses from double taxation and ensuring the portability of their pension benefits, thereby legally securing the talent pipeline.

Conclusion

The India–Italy Special Strategic Partnership marks a decisive shift in bilateral relations from traditional diplomacy to a multidimensional strategic alliance. With cooperation spanning defence, trade, AI, energy, maritime security, and connectivity, both nations are positioning themselves as key partners in shaping an emerging Indo-Mediterranean order.

Sperm Whale

★ ★ ☆ ☆ ☆

Source: TH

A recent Proceedings of the Royal Society B study found that sperm whale communication is more complex than earlier understood, with sound categories, timing, and sequencing rules resembling human phonology.

  • It suggests that humans and sperm whales may have independently evolved complex vocal systems due to their advanced social behaviour.

Sperm Whales

  • About: Sperm whales are the largest toothed whales, generally dark grey to brownish in colour, with some individuals having white patches on the belly.
    • They have an extremely large head, making up nearly one-third of their body length, and are the only living cetaceans with a single blowhole placed asymmetrically on the left side of the head.
    • They are named after spermaceti, a waxy oil sac in their heads that helps focus sound, and they possess the largest brain of any animal.
  • Diet: Sperm whales feed mainly on deep-sea species such as squid, sharks, skates and fish, consuming around 3 to 3.5% of their body weight daily.
  • Reproduction & Growth: Female sperm whales mature around 9 years of age, give birth to a single calf after a 14–16 month gestation period, and reproduce once every 5–7 years.
    • Male sperm whales grow much larger than females, mature physically around 50 years, and usually begin active breeding only in their late twenties.
    • Females live in stable family-based social units in tropical waters, while young males leave to form bachelor pods, and older males often migrate toward polar regions or live alone.
  • Distribution: Sperm whales have one of the widest global distributions among marine mammals, inhabiting deep oceans from the equator to the edges of Arctic and Antarctic pack ice .
    • Their distribution and migration depend on food availability, breeding conditions, sex and age , with adult males often migrating to temperate waters while females and young usually remain in tropical waters year-round.
  • Conservation Status:
    • IUCN: Vulnerable
    • CITES: Appendix I
    • Convention on Migratory Species (CMS): Appendix I
    • Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972 : Schedule I
  • Threats: Major threats include vessel strikes, entanglement in fishing gear, ocean noise, marine debris, and climate-driven changes in habitat and food availability.

Gaganyaan’s Environmental Control and Life Support System

★ ★ ☆ ☆ ☆

Source: TH

Indian Space Research Organisation Gaganyaan Mission aims to send Indian astronauts into a 400-km low Earth orbit. To ensure astronaut survival in space, the spacecraft is equipped with an advanced Environmental Control and Life Support System (ECLSS) that recreates Earth-like living conditions by regulating air, water, pressure, temperature, humidity, and waste management in a microgravity environment.

  • Gaganyaan is a short-duration mission , so supplies like oxygen and water are carried from Earth and waste is stored for disposal later. Long-duration missions recycle waste into breathable air and clean water.

Core Subsystems of an ECLSS

  • Air Revitalisation: The Air Revitalisation System (ARS) supplies fresh oxygen and actively removes exhaled carbon dioxide using lithium hydroxide canisters with activated charcoal .
    • Small ECLSS fans circulate air to prevent lethal pockets of CO 2 and oxygen from forming due to the lack of natural convection in microgravity.
  • Thermal and Humidity Regulation: The cabin maintains a strict temperature of 20-26°C and 30-70% humidity .
    • Active cooling systems use heat exchangers to expel metabolic and electronic heat into space , while condensing units manage moisture to prevent short-circuits and microbial growth.
  • Atmospheric Pressure: The cabin pressure is strictly maintained at 101.3 kilopascal (kPa) to mimic Earth’s sea-level conditions , utilizing electronic sensors and safety valves to balance breathable gas levels.
  • Water Management: Due to microgravity, water forms floating globules that threaten electronics and pose inhalation hazards.
    • Gaganyaan utilizes pressurized bladders to mechanically force potable water from specially designed pouches directly to the astronauts.
  • Waste Disposal: Microgravity necessitates suction-based airflow systems to manage human waste, preventing it from floating.
    • Faeces and urine are collected, chemically treated to inhibit bacterial growth and toxic ammonia buildup , and stored in sealed containers for Earth disposal.
  • Fire Suppression: Since microgravity causes fires to expand spherically, Gaganyaan uses smoke detectors and fine water mist fire extinguishers , which effectively cool the fire while simultaneously scrubbing toxic smoke particles from the cabin air.

Recently, Tamil Nadu’s Chief Minister paid floral tributes to Pandit Iyothee Thass on his 181 st birth anniversary .

Pandit Iyothee Thass

  • About: Pandit Iyothee Thass was a prominent social reformer, thinker, anti-caste activist and pioneer of the Dalit Buddhist movement in South India .
  • Birth: He was born on 20 th May 1845 in Thousand Lights, Madras , in the Madras Presidency during British rule.
  • Anti-Caste Movement: He worked extensively against caste discrimination and untouchability , advocating dignity and equal rights for oppressed communities.
  • Buddhist Revival: He argued that oppressed castes were originally Buddhists and promoted the revival of Buddhism among Dalits in Tamil society.
  • Organisations: As a young reformer, he organised the Depressed Classes through the Advaidananda Sabha , which opposed religious conversion pressures and explored Advaitic ideas to challenge caste discrimination.
    • He later led the Dravida Mahajana Sabha in 1891 and established the Sakya Buddhist Society in Madras in 1898, strengthening social awareness, anti-caste mobilisation and Buddhist revival among oppressed communities.
  • Census Demand: In the context of the 1881 Census, he demanded that oppressed and outcaste communities be identified as Original Tamils rather than classified as Hindus.
  • Journalism: He launched the Tamil journal Oru Paisa Tamizhan in 1907, later renamed Tamilan , as a key platform for social reform and anti-caste discourse.
  • Significance: He is regarded as one of the earliest voices of social justice, self-respect and Dalit assertion in modern India .

India’s Abstention on UNGA Climate Resolution

★ ★ ☆ ☆ ☆

Source:TH

India abstained from voting on a United Nations General Assembly (UNGA) resolution that calls on states to comply with climate change obligations based on an advisory opinion of the International Court of Justice (ICJ).

  • Voting Outcome: The resolution, originally moved by the Pacific island nation of Vanuatu, was adopted by the 193-member UNGA with 141 votes in favor, 8 against (including the US and Russia), and 28 abstentions (including India) .
  • ICJ Advisory Opinion on Climate Change: Ruled that countries have an explicit legal obligation to combat anthropogenic climate change and protect environmental systems for both present and future generations .
    • The ICJ ruled that a state's Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) fall under the principle of due diligence. Countries must exercise strict administrative enforcement and ensure their climate goals represent their "highest possible ambition".
  • Legal Status of the Opinion: While an ICJ advisory opinion is non-binding, it carries substantial "legal weight and moral authority" that will shape the evolution of customary international environmental law and domestic climate litigation globally.

Key Reasons Behind India's Abstention

  • Defending the UNFCCC's "Sacrosanct Architecture": India expressed serious concern that the resolution attempts to elevate a non-binding ICJ advisory opinion to a binding or quasi-binding status.
    • India asserted that its climate obligations arise solely from outcomes negotiated under the UNFCCC , not from judicial mandates.
  • Protecting NDCs: The resolution prescribes specific mitigation pathways and external benchmarks.
    • India argued that this threatens the bottom-up, nationally determined architecture of the Paris Agreement and exposes domestic climate targets (NDCs) to international judicial or quasi-judicial scrutiny.
  • Glaring Omission of Climate Finance: India strongly objected to the complete absence of the term " climate finance " in the text.
    • India's representative termed it a "serious omission," highlighting that the resolution imposes sweeping obligations without reaffirming the fundamental duty of developed countries to provide predictable financing, technology transfer, and capacity building.
  • Dilution of CBDR-RC & Just Transition: The draft failed to adequately distinguish the responsibilities of historical emitters from developing economies.
    • India reiterated the core principle of Common But Differentiated Responsibilities and Respective Capabilities (CBDR-RC) , insisting that any energy transition must be just, orderly, and equitable , ensuring it does not derail poverty eradication and economic growth in the Global South.
  • Solidarity with Small Island Developing States (SIDS): Despite these foundational disagreements, India deliberately chose to abstain rather than vote against the resolution (unlike the US or Russia).
    • This move signaled India's continued solidarity with the existential climate vulnerabilities and "historical injustice" faced by SIDS like Vanuatu.

Mission Arunachal Kiwi

★ ★ ☆ ☆ ☆

Source: PIB

Recently, the Ministry of Development of North Eastern Region (MDoNER) launched the Mission on “Arunachal Kiwi: The Unique Selling Proposition of Arunachal Pradesh” to promote Arunachal Pradesh as India’s premium organic kiwi hub under the Viksit North East vision.

  • About: The Mission has an outlay of around ₹167 crore, follows a whole-of-government, convergence-led model anchored by the MDoNER and supported by key ministries, institutions and private investors.
    • The mission forms part of the broader “One State–One USP” approach , including products such as Lakadong Turmeric (Meghalaya), Coffee (Nagaland), Ginger (Mizoram) and Muga Silk (Assam) .
  • Objectives: The mission aims to address key gaps such as low farmer prices, distress sales, weak cold-chain infrastructure , lapsed National Programme for Organic Production certification, poor branding and limited export linkages.
  • Cluster-Based Model: The programme adopts a cluster-based approach with six integrated post-harvest management hubs across Ziro Valley, Dirang, Kalaktang, Shi Yomi and Dibang Valley .
  • Strategic Pillars: Its four strategic pillars are convergence, value addition, branding and market integration, with targets such as 2,000 metric tonnes cold-chain capacity, improved processing and higher farmer income.
  • Market Opportunity: Arunachal’s November–January harvest season coincides with New Zealand’s off-season , creating export opportunities in South-East Asia, Europe and the Middle East . Arunachal Pradesh produces over 50% of India’s kiwi output and was the 1 st state to receive organic kiwi certification.
  • Significance: The initiative aims to reduce post-harvest losses, increase farmer income, strengthen organic agriculture and position “Arunachal Organic Kiwi” in premium global markets .
